The key insight here is that for structured, tabular data with limited rows, gradient boosting methods like XGBoost consistently match or exceed deep learning performance while training orders of magnitude faster. We benchmarked both on our customer churn dataset — 8,400 rows, 23 features. XGBoost reached 0.94 AUC in 12 seconds. The neural net took 8 minutes and plateaued at 0.87 AUC. This pattern holds across most tabular tasks under 10K rows, as confirmed by multiple Kaggle competition analyses.

Real-time ML inference adds value but demands operational maturity
Evaluating real-time vs. batch scoring for production recommendation engine
✓ Advantages
✔ Sub-100ms latency enables personalized results during active sessions
✔ Adapts to real-time behavioral signals (clicks, cart adds, dwell time)
✔ A/B tests show 23% higher CTR vs. batch-scored recommendations
✔ Enables contextual features like time-of-day and device type
✗ Trade-offs
✘ Requires dedicated serving infrastructure (adds $4K/month)
✘ Needs 99.9% uptime SLA with fallback to cached predictions
✘ Model monitoring is more complex with streaming data drift
✘ Team needs MLOps skills beyond current batch-only expertise
This is a balanced view for our engineering leadership. The advantages are clear — real-time inference gave us a 23% improvement in click-through rate in our A/B test last quarter. But the trade-offs are real. We estimate $4K per month in additional infrastructure costs for a model serving layer, and our team will need to develop MLOps capabilities we don't currently have. My recommendation: start with a hybrid approach. Batch-score 80% of users and reserve real-time inference for high-value sessions where the CTR lift justifies the cost.
