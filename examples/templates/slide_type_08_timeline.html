Walk through each stage left to right. Stage 1 ingests data from our three primary sources — the product database, the event stream, and third-party enrichment APIs. Stage 2 applies Great Expectations validation suites and dbt transformations. Stage 3 is where the data science team does their core work: feature engineering and model training using our standardized experiment tracking in MLflow. This timeline tells the story of our data science organization's growth. We started with a single analyst doing SQL queries and dashboards in Q1 2023. The inflection point was Q3 2023 when that analyst built a churn prediction model as a proof of concept — it saved us $240K in one quarter, which made the business case for building a dedicated team. By Q1 2024 we had four people, and we used Q3 2024 to build out our MLOps platform so we could scale. Today, we're at 12 people with six models in production driving $2.8 million in measurable revenue impact. The key lesson: start with one high-impact proof of concept before asking for headcount.

MLOps is not a pipeline — it is a cycle. Starting at the top: we Monitor production models for data drift and performance degradation using Evidently. When we detect issues, we Analyze root causes — is it a data quality problem, a schema change, or genuine distribution shift? That analysis triggers a Retrain phase where we pull updated data and run our training pipeline. Before anything goes live, we Validate with shadow-mode A/B tests. We Deploy using blue-green deployments so we can roll back instantly. And crucially, we close the loop with Feedback — capturing user signals like click-through rates and conversion to inform the next monitoring cycle. The teams that skip this last step end up retraining on stale assumptions.
