Our gradient boosting model version 3.2 shows strong performance on the Q1 evaluation dataset of nearly 43,000 customers. Precision is 0.94, meaning when the model predicts churn, it is correct 94% of the time — well above our 0.90 target. Recall is 0.91, so we are catching 91% of actual churners. F1-Score at 0.92 confirms the balance between precision and recall. AUC-ROC at 0.87 is the one metric below target — this suggests the model's ranking ability across all thresholds could improve, likely by adding more temporal features. That said, the operating point metrics are excellent for production deployment.

The confusion matrix shows our fraud detection model's performance on the test set of 11,000 transactions. The diagonal — true negatives and true positives — dominates, which is what we want. We correctly identified 9,412 legitimate transactions and 1,372 fraudulent ones. The 127 false positives represent a 1.3% false positive rate, meaning only 1 in 75 legitimate transactions gets incorrectly flagged. The 89 false negatives are the ones that concern us most — these are fraudulent transactions we missed. That gives us a recall of 93.9%, meaning we catch nearly 94 out of every 100 fraud cases. The combined F1-Score of 92.7% puts us well above the 90% threshold required for production deployment.

This table compares three candidate models for the customer lifetime value prediction task. XGBoost wins decisively on accuracy, F1-Score, and AUC-ROC — achieving 0.923 accuracy compared to Random Forest's 0.891 and Logistic Regression's 0.847. The AUC-ROC gap is especially notable at 0.961 versus 0.934 for Random Forest. However, Logistic Regression has the fastest inference time at 2.1 milliseconds, which matters for our real-time serving use case. XGBoost at 5.7 milliseconds is still well within our 15-millisecond SLA, making it the clear winner. We will proceed with XGBoost for production deployment, with Logistic Regression as our fallback for latency-sensitive edge cases.
