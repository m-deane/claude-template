Population Stability Index flagged three features with PSI above the 0.25 critical threshold

drift_monitor.py 
from evidently.metrics import DataDriftTable
from evidently import Report

# Compare training vs production distributions
drift_report = Report(metrics=[
    DataDriftTable(
        num_stattest="psi",
        cat_stattest="chisquare",
        num_stattest_threshold=0.25,
    )
])

drift_report.run(
    reference_data=train_df,
    current_data=prod_df_q4,
)

# Results: 3 features above PSI threshold
# avg_session_duration:  PSI = 0.41
# competitor_offer_count: PSI = 0.38
# contract_months_left:  PSI = 0.31

Root Cause

A competitor launched a promotional campaign in September. The distribution of competitor_offer_count shifted from a mean of 1.2 to 3.8 offers per customer.

PSI Interpretation

PSI above 0.25 indicates significant distribution shift. Three features crossed this threshold, all related to customer engagement patterns.

Fix Required

Retrain on Q3-Q4 data with expanded competitor features. Add automated PSI monitoring with Slack alerts at 0.20 threshold.

Here is the drift detection code that surfaced the problem. We use Evidently's DataDriftTable with PSI, the Population Stability Index, as our statistical test for numerical features and chi-square for categorical ones. The PSI threshold of 0.25 is the standard critical threshold, anything above it means the feature distribution has shifted materially. Our monitoring found three features well above that line. Average session duration hit a PSI of 0.41. Competitor offer count reached 0.38, and contract months remaining hit 0.31. The root cause is clear: a major competitor launched an aggressive promotional campaign in September. The distribution of competitor offers per customer nearly tripled from a mean of 1.2 to 3.8. Our model was trained on a world where customers rarely saw competing offers, and that world no longer exists. The fix is straightforward but requires engineering time: retrain on fresh Q3-Q4 data that captures this new competitive landscape, and add automated PSI monitoring so we catch this earlier next time.

This diagram shows our target architecture organized into three layers. On the left, the data layer. Our existing sources — Kafka for streaming events, S3 for batch data, PostgreSQL for transactional data — stay the same. But we are adding a Feast feature store with a Redis online store. This is the single most important infrastructure addition because it ensures that training and production features are computed identically, which eliminates the training-serving skew that contributed to the churn model's drift. In the middle, the ML layer. We are upgrading our MLflow deployment with proper model versioning and lineage tracking, so we can trace any production model back to its exact training data and hyperparameters. The new XAI module uses SHAP for tree-based models like the fraud detector and pricing model, and Alibi counterfactual explanations for the autoencoder. This is the component that satisfies the new regulatory explainability requirement before the April audit. On the right, the serving layer. We are migrating the anomaly autoencoder from raw PyTorch to ONNX Runtime, which should cut its latency from 84 milliseconds to under 25 based on our benchmarks with a comparable architecture. And we are upgrading monitoring to include automated PSI alerts through PagerDuty so drift gets caught within days rather than months. The green-tagged components are net new, the yellow-tagged ones are upgrades to existing infrastructure.
